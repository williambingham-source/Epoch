\section*{Examples}

\subsection*{Example 1: $\alpha = \varphi^{-1} = (\sqrt{5}-1)/2 \approx 0.6180$}

The golden ratio $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ has continued fraction
$[1; 1, 1, 1, \ldots]$, the slowest-converging of all irrationals. Its
reciprocal $\alpha = \varphi^{-1} = \varphi - 1$ is the ``most irrational''
number in a precise sense, and gives the most balanced three-distance
configurations.

\medskip
\noindent The Fibonacci numbers $F_k$ are the denominators of the convergents to
$\varphi^{-1}$:
\[
  F_1=1,\; F_2=1,\; F_3=2,\; F_4=3,\; F_5=5,\; F_6=8,\; F_7=13,\;\ldots
\]
At $n = F_k$ the $n$ points form \emph{two} distinct gap lengths; for
$F_k < n < F_{k+1}$ there are exactly \emph{three}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Small cases:}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cll}
\hline
$n$ & Gap lengths & Notes \\
\hline
1 & $\{0.618, 0.382\}$ & 2 lengths \\
2 & $\{0.382, 0.236, 0.382\} \to \{0.382, 0.236\}$ & 2 lengths ($n=F_3-1$)\\
3 & $\{0.382, 0.236, 0.146, 0.236\} \to \{0.382, 0.236, 0.146\}$ & 3 lengths \\
  & $0.382 = 0.236 + 0.146$ & sum condition \\
5 & 5 gaps, 2 distinct lengths & $n = F_5$ \\
6 & 6 gaps, 3 distinct lengths & \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection*{Example 2: $\alpha = \sqrt{2} - 1 \approx 0.4142$}

The continued fraction of $\sqrt{2}$ is $[1; 2, 2, 2, \ldots]$, giving
convergents with denominators $1, 2, 5, 12, 29, 70, \ldots$ (the Pell numbers).
At $n = 5$:
\[
  S_5(\sqrt{2}-1) \;=\; \{0.414, 0.828, 0.243, 0.657, 0.071\}.
\]
Sorting: $0.071, 0.243, 0.414, 0.657, 0.828$. The five gaps (including the
wrap-around from $0.828$ to $1+0.071$) are:
\[
  0.172,\; 0.171,\; 0.243,\; 0.171,\; 0.243.
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  \lambda_1 \approx 0.171,\; \lambda_2 \approx 0.172,\; \lambda_3 \approx 0.243.
\]
(The small discrepancy between $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ is due to rounding;
exactly, $\lambda_1 = \lambda_2$ and there are only two distinct lengths at
$n = 5$, consistent with $5$ being a Pell denominator.)

\subsection*{Connection to continued fractions}

In general, write $\alpha = [0; a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots]$ with convergents
$p_k/q_k$. The three gap lengths at step $n$ are always
\[
  \alpha_k \;=\; \{q_k \alpha\},\qquad
  \alpha_{k-1} \;=\; \{q_{k-1}\alpha\},\qquad
  \alpha_k + \alpha_{k-1},
\]
where $k$ is chosen so that $q_k \le n < q_{k+1}$. This gives a complete
description of the gap structure for any $n$ directly from the continued
fraction expansion of $\alpha$.
